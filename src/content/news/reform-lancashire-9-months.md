On 1 May 2025, Reform UK won 53 of 84 seats at Lancashire County Council, the first time any party outside the Conservatives or Labour had controlled a county council in England. Nine months later, the results are in the accounts, in the roads, and in the services that residents actually use.

This is not a political sales pitch. It is a comparison: what we inherited versus what we have done, measured in the same units the council has always used.

## The Budget: What You Pay, What You Get

The Conservatives raised council tax by the legal maximum (4.99%) in seven of their eight years. They treated the referendum cap as a target rather than a limit.

Reform's first budget, passed in February 2026, set the council tax increase at **3.80%**, the lowest rise in Lancashire in 12 years. The breakdown:

- **2.00%** for general council services
- **1.80%** for the Adult Social Care precept

Band D residents now pay &pound;1,514.19 for the county share of their council tax. Still too high. But the direction has changed.

## The Overspend: What We Inherited

When Reform took control, the council faced a **&pound;28.3 million overspend** for 2024/25. The Conservatives had set a budget they could not deliver.

Nine months later, the budget is on track. The savings programme for 2025/26 is hitting **100% of its targets**, compared to 48% under the last full year of Conservative control.

That is not rhetoric. It is the quarterly monitoring report, published on the council's website, measured by the same finance team that served both administrations.

## Roads: The Backlog and What We Have Done About It

The Conservatives left Lancashire with a **&pound;650 million highways maintenance backlog**. That figure comes from the council's own highways condition assessment. It is not a number Reform invented.

In the first six months of Reform control, reported potholes fell by **42%**. The council deployed AI-equipped sensors on bin lorries to identify road defects before residents had to report them. A new three-year &pound;45 million resurfacing programme targets the 100 worst roads in the county.

The backlog is not fixed. It will take years. But the trend has reversed and the approach is different: proactive rather than reactive, data-driven rather than complaint-driven.

## Social Care: What Matters Most

The outgoing Conservative administration announced plans to close five county care homes. Reform cancelled those closures. All five homes remain open.

This was not a populist gesture. The council ran a full public consultation with 1,600 respondents. The consultation showed that the homes served residents who had nowhere else to go. The business case for closure was built on assumptions that did not account for the real cost of placing those residents in private care.

In children's services, Lancashire received a Department for Education improvement notice under the Conservatives. Reform's response: 677 new SEND school places commissioned, the EHCP backlog programme accelerated, and SEND transport costs reduced by competitive tendering.

## Financial Damage: What They Left Behind

The full scale of the Conservative financial record is documented separately in our analysis of the Statement of Accounts. The headline figures:

- **&pound;416.9 million** in treasury and financial instrument losses
- **&pound;254.5 million** in disposal and academy transfer losses
- **&pound;138.4 million** in department overspends
- **&pound;111.7 million** in direct subsidy and partnership costs

Total documented financial damage: **&pound;921.5 million** over eight years.

When the estimated unrealised loss on the &pound;600 million UKMBA bond portfolio is included, the total rises to **&pound;1.27 billion**.

These are not Reform UK calculations. They are the council's own audited figures, sourced to specific pages in the Statement of Accounts from 2017/18 to 2024/25.

## Technology: How the Council Works Now

The Conservatives ran Lancashire County Council on paper forms and spreadsheets. Reform invested &pound;4.3 million in the Netcall AI platform, now used by over 1,400 council officers.

The platform handles 72% of initial customer contacts without human intervention. Residents get answers faster. Staff spend their time on cases that actually need human judgement.

AI-equipped sensors on bin lorries scan road surfaces during normal collection rounds, identifying defects before they become complaints. The data feeds directly into the highways maintenance programme.

None of this is expensive or experimental. It is the application of technology that has been standard in the private sector for years but that local government has been slow to adopt.

## What Comes Next

Nine months is not enough to fix eight years of neglect. The &pound;650 million highways backlog will take a decade. The SEND improvement notice requires sustained investment. The financial instrument losses cannot be recovered.

But the trajectory has changed. The council tax rise is lower. The budget is balanced. The savings programme is delivering. The roads are measurably better. The care homes are open. The technology works.
