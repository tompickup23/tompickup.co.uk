East Lancashire has a waste crisis. Not because the bins aren't collected, but because the people in charge spent a decade failing to decide where the rubbish goes after collection. That failure has cost taxpayers tens of millions of pounds.

In December 2025, Reform put waste disposal out to **open competitive tender for the first time in a generation**. The result: a &pound;60.3 million contract with Envirofuel (SRF) Ltd, processing waste locally in Burnley instead of trucking it 25 miles to Farington near Leyland. Lower cost. Lower emissions. Better value.

The Conservatives approved the planning permission that made this possible. Then one of their councillors called it "so wrong."

Here are the facts.

Waste disposal alone cost **&pound;88.3 million** in 2024-25. The running total was &pound;105.9 million, offset by &pound;17.7 million in income. Without competitive tendering, these costs would have kept climbing.

Published spend data reveals **over &pound;78 million in LCC waste spending across more than 1,100 transactions** &mdash; and every single one has an empty payment description. That is a 100% vague description rate. The largest transparency black hole in Lancashire's &pound;3.6 billion spending record.

## How We Got Here: Twenty Years of Failure

**2006&ndash;2014: The &pound;2 billion PFI that collapsed.** LCC pursued a Private Finance Initiative for county-wide waste treatment. After eight years and millions in consultancy fees, the preferred bidder withdrew. No backup plan existed.

**2014&ndash;2023: Dependency on an ageing landfill.** With no alternative, LCC kept sending East Lancashire waste to Whinney Hill in Altham &mdash; a site operational since the 1970s, generating persistent complaints about smell, seagulls, and vermin. SUEZ, the operator, received **&pound;57.7 million** from LCC over this period. A temporary measure that lasted a decade.

**2023: A &pound;600 million procurement, paused.** LCC paused an Energy from Waste procurement, delaying any permanent solution further. The Conservatives did, however, approve planning permission for Envirofuel's gasification facility at Hapton Valley (application LCC/2023/0024). This becomes important later.

**December 2023: Another one-year extension.** LCC extended the SUEZ contract at Whinney Hill to March 2026. Borough councils were told to sort themselves out.

**August 2025: Burnley's Heasandford plan, delayed.** Burnley Council approved a waste transfer station at Heasandford Industrial Estate (&pound;361,000 land purchase), projected to save &pound;1.6 million per year. Labour's Cllr Mark Townsend called the decision in to scrutiny. The Conservative chair abstained. Implementation stalled.

The cost of all this indecision? **&pound;8&ndash;10 million per year** in additional costs spread across Burnley, Hyndburn, Pendle, and Rossendale.

In December 2025, Reform put the contract out to **open competitive tender** via the Find a Tender Service &mdash; the first time East Lancashire waste disposal had been openly tendered in recent memory.

The contract runs for two years (April 2026&ndash;March 2028) with an option to extend by up to 24 months. Each lot received two tenders, assessed on both quality and price.

**Why this is better value:** waste is processed locally in Burnley, not transported 25+ miles to Farington. Lower haulage costs. Lower carbon emissions. Waste converted to Solid Recovered Fuel for cement kilns rather than rotting in landfill. And the facility has been operating safely for over 25 years, holding a current Environment Agency permit.

## The &pound;78 Million Transparency Black Hole

Published spend data reveals something striking about LCC's waste spending. Across every waste-related department &mdash; Whinney Hill landfill, waste management, hazardous waste, clinical waste, recycling &mdash; there are **over 1,100 transactions totalling more than &pound;78 million, all with a 100% vague description rate**.

Every single payment description is empty. Not one of these transactions tells the public what the money was actually spent on.

The Whinney Hill landfill alone accounts for **&pound;73.6 million across 374 transactions** &mdash; all with blank descriptions. SUEZ received &pound;57.7 million in total from LCC, with large same-day payment clusters including &pound;5.9 million on a single day in June 2024.

This is the kind of opacity that allowed costs to spiral unchecked for a decade. When nobody can see what's being paid for, nobody can challenge whether it represents value for money.

## The Consistency Question

In November 2023, LCC's Development Control Committee considered planning application LCC/2023/0024 for Envirofuel's gasification facility at Hapton Valley. Cllr Jamie McGowan (Conservative, Burnley) spoke against it, citing pollution concerns, and asked the committee to defer for a site visit.

The committee deferred, then approved the application under the **Conservative-led county council**. This planning permission expanded Envirofuel's capacity and made the 2025 waste contract technically possible.

In December 2025, Reform awarded the waste contract to Envirofuel through open competitive tender. In January 2026, Cllr McGowan called the deal "so wrong."

The facility that his own party approved is the same facility he now objects to being used.

Burnley Council officers had been informed of the forthcoming tender in September 2025 &mdash; approximately four months before McGowan's public intervention. Under the Public Contracts Regulations 2015, LCC was legally unable to make any public announcement until after the mandatory standstill period elapsed and the contract was formally signed. LCC followed the law.

## Burnley's Own &pound;37.6 Million Question

While scrutinising LCC's transparent procurement, it is worth examining Burnley Borough Council's own waste arrangements.

Burnley awarded an **8-year waste collection contract to FCC Environment (2026&ndash;2034)** worth approximately &pound;4.7 million per year &mdash; totalling &pound;37.6 million &mdash; via a **negotiated procedure without open competitive tender**.

The Public Contracts Regulations 2015 require services contracts exceeding the threshold (&pound;214,904 for local authorities) to be advertised through the Find a Tender Service.

Analysis of comparable Lancashire authorities shows **8&ndash;12% cost reductions** achieved through competitive tendering. Even a 5% saving on this contract would be worth &pound;1.88 million over eight years. A 10% saving would deliver &pound;3.76 million back to Burnley taxpayers.

Published contract data also shows Burnley's previous Urbaser VEAT contract (&pound;2.7 million) as a **weak competition anomaly**: it was awarded with a tender period of negative four days and zero days for evaluation. The contract was signed before the tender deadline.

## The Bottom Line

East Lancashire's waste crisis is the product of twenty years of institutional failure. A collapsed &pound;2 billion PFI. A paused &pound;600 million procurement. Expired landfill contracts. Borough-level delays. And over &pound;78 million in spending with zero transparency.

Reform acted. The tender was open. The law was followed. The taxpayer benefits.
