The government wants to abolish Lancashire's 15 councils and replace them with two or three new unitary authorities. The consultation closes on 26 March. A decision is expected by September. Vesting day - the day the new councils legally come into existence - is pencilled in for April 2028.

That gives 18 months from decision to go-live. It is the most compressed LGR timeline ever proposed for a county of this size.

What nobody in Whitehall appears to have grappled with is what happens to the contracts.

## The Scale of the Problem

Lancashire's councils hold an estimated 3,200 active contracts with a combined value in the region of £3.7 billion. That figure comes from Contracts Finder data across all 15 authorities plus LCC's own procurement pipeline, published in its March 2026 cabinet papers.

LCC alone has 39 procurement exercises in progress or planned, worth over £3.3 billion. Twenty-eight of those contracts will be live on the proposed vesting day. Eight more will be awarded during the transition period - meaning an outgoing authority will be signing deals that bind successor councils for years to come.

Each of those contracts needs to be novated - legally transferred - to whichever successor authority inherits the relevant service. That is not a paperwork exercise. Novation requires the supplier's consent. It often triggers TUPE obligations for staff employed under the contract. It can require re-executed schedules, updated insurance, and fresh performance bonds. If a supplier objects, the whole thing goes to negotiation or, worse, litigation.

Dorset Council went through this in 2019. Nine councils merging into two. Roughly 800 contracts. It took 24 months. Lancashire has four to five times the complexity.

## The Mega-Contracts

Three LCC contracts dwarf everything else. Together they are worth £2.28 billion and they all cross the proposed vesting day.

The SEND Schools Partnership is worth £920 million over six years, starting September 2026. It will run until 2032 - four years past vesting. The SEND Transition to Adulthood contract is £660 million over ten years, starting April 2027. That start date is vesting day itself, meaning it will be awarded by the outgoing county council and inherited by authorities that had no say in the procurement. The Residential Accommodation contract for older people is £700 million over eight years, starting October 2028 - six months after vesting, procured during whatever transition chaos is happening at the time.

These are not discretionary services. SEND provision is a statutory obligation. If there is a gap in service - even a brief one - parents have legal recourse through judicial review. Lancashire already manages over 15,000 Education, Health and Care Plans. Every one of those has to transfer seamlessly.

## Highways: Three Authorities, One Road Network

Lancashire is unusual in having three highway authorities: LCC manages 7,142 kilometres of road across the 12 districts, while Blackpool and Blackburn with Darwen each manage their own networks as existing unitaries.

LCC has nine highways contracts in its current pipeline, worth £122.3 million. They cover everything from A-road resurfacing to traffic signal maintenance. Several are structured in geographic lots - North, South, East - that do not align with any of the proposed successor authority boundaries.

The A Roads Resurfacing contract, for instance, is lotted North/South/East with a combined value of £14.7 million. If the two-unitary model is chosen, splitting along the Ribble, the existing lot structure becomes meaningless. The contracts would need complete restructuring, not simple novation.

Then there is the DfT highways settlement. LCC secured a four-year deal worth £231.8 million from 2026 to 2030. Under the current timeline, vesting in April 2028 means two years of that settlement would need to transfer mid-term to successor authorities. No English county has ever split a multi-year DfT settlement like this. If the Department does not honour the full amount to the new councils, Lancashire faces a funding gap that could run to tens of millions.

Traffic signal maintenance is another quiet crisis in waiting. LCC is responsible for 5,778 signals. The maintenance contract is £7 million over four years. Signals are safety-critical infrastructure - there cannot be a gap in maintenance while lawyers argue about who inherits the contract.

## The District Contracts Nobody Talks About

The focus tends to fall on LCC because its contracts are the largest. But the 12 district councils collectively hold hundreds of contracts that also need dealing with.

Every district has its own waste collection contract, typically worth £2 to £8 million per year on terms of seven to ten years. These have to integrate with LCC's waste disposal arrangements. Leisure services are often outsourced to trusts on 10 to 25-year terms. Revenues and benefits contracts handle council tax billing and housing benefit - the plumbing of local government finance.

Lancaster is a revealing case. It has zero presence on Contracts Finder - not a single published notice. That does not mean it has no contracts. It means its procurement is below the threshold or simply not published. This is true of several smaller districts. The real contract count across Lancashire's boroughs is almost certainly higher than the 1,222 notices that appear on the public register.

Burnley, by contrast, has 94 notices on Contracts Finder with £49 million in awarded contracts. The single largest is the Liberata outsourcing deal at £36.7 million - a comprehensive back-office arrangement that covers revenues, benefits, and corporate services. Unpicking that from a merged authority structure is not straightforward.

## IT: The Contract That Binds Everything

LCC is in the process of awarding three IT contracts that will shape the technology landscape for whichever councils come next.

ICT Managed Services: £68 million over five years, starting June 2026. This is the IT backbone - servers, networks, service desk, the lot. The ERP/Finance System: £45 million over seven years, replacing the failed SAP implementation with a system that runs until 2033. Revenues and Benefits: £40 million over five years, the Northgate platform that handles council tax for the entire county.

That is £153 million in IT commitments being made by an authority that will not exist to see them through. The successor councils will inherit whatever system is chosen, whatever supplier is appointed, whatever terms are agreed. They will have had no input into the specification, the evaluation, or the negotiation.

Buckinghamshire went through this in 2020. Five councils into one. Three years after vesting, it was still running parallel finance systems. The estimated excess cost was £8 to £12 million.

Lancashire is three times the scale.

## The Workforce Question

This is not only about contracts with external suppliers. LCC employs roughly 23,000 people. Add the 12 district councils, Blackpool, and Blackburn, and the total is north of 30,000.

Every one of those staff members is subject to TUPE - the Transfer of Undertakings regulations that protect employees when their employer changes. TUPE requires 90-day consultation periods, job evaluation, and ultimately pay harmonisation. North Yorkshire, which reorganised in 2023, found that TUPE was the single longest item on its critical path. It took 18 months on its own.

The equal pay risk is material. When Northamptonshire reorganised, the failure to properly harmonise pay resulted in claims estimated at £15 to £30 million. Lancashire has more staff, more councils, and wider pay differentials between districts.

## The Timeline Does Not Work

The government's 18-month timeline from decision to vesting assumes that all of this - contract novation, IT integration, TUPE transfer, property division, budget setting, service redesign, pension restructuring - can happen concurrently and at speed.

The longest workstreams - IT integration and pensions - each require 18 months of work. But they cannot start until the legal framework is in place, which itself takes six months. That means the absolute minimum is 24 months. The proposed timeline is 18.

Previous LGR transitions confirm this. North Yorkshire had eight councils and 620,000 people. It took 24 months. Dorset had nine councils and a similar population. It took 24 months. Lancashire has 15 councils and 1.5 million people. It is structurally the most complex reorganisation ever attempted in England.

A 30-month timeline - decision in September 2026, shadow elections in May 2028, vesting in April 2029 - would cost more in the short term due to 12 extra months of running the existing structure. But the analysis suggests it saves £14 to £34 million overall by avoiding the litigation, service disruption, and botched novations that come with rushing.

## What Should Happen

The contractual reality needs to drive the political timetable, not the other way around.

First, there should be a full contract audit across all 15 councils before any vesting date is fixed. Not a desk exercise - a proper review of every contract that crosses the transition period, with novation risk ratings and supplier engagement.

Second, the three mega-contracts - SEND Schools, SEND Adulthood, and Residential Care - need specific transition plans. These are services for vulnerable people. Getting them wrong is not an administrative inconvenience. It is a safeguarding failure.

Third, the DfT highways settlement needs a written guarantee from government that successor authorities will receive the full £231.8 million. Without that, Lancashire's roads - already carrying a £650 million maintenance backlog - face a funding cliff.

Fourth, the IT contracts being awarded now should include explicit break clauses and novation provisions for LGR. If LCC is signing seven-year deals in 2026, those deals need to anticipate the structural change that everyone knows is coming.

And fifth, the timeline needs to reflect reality. Eighteen months is not enough. The precedents say 24 months minimum. The complexity of Lancashire says 30.
