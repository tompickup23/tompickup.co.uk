On 7 May, voters in all 15 Burnley wards will elect one councillor each. These elections very nearly did not happen.

## The Attempt to Cancel Your Vote

In December 2025, the Labour government wrote to councils undergoing Local Government Reorganisation asking whether they wanted their May 2026 elections postponed. On 13 January 2026, Burnley's Executive met and voted unanimously to request cancellation. The five Executive members who voted for postponement were Cllr Afrasiab Anwar (Independent, Leader), Cllr Margaret Lishman (Lib Dem, Deputy Leader), Cllr Lubna Khan (Independent), Cllr Howard Baker (Lib Dem) and Cllr Jack Launer (Green). This was despite the Scrutiny Committee, which met earlier the same evening, recommending the opposite.

The government then confirmed it would legislate to postpone elections in 29 council areas, including Burnley. In response, Reform UK launched a judicial review. On 16 February 2026, in the face of that legal challenge, the government reversed its position and reinstated all elections.

During the cancellation period, I wrote to all 15 councillors whose seats were due for election, asking whether any would voluntarily resign to trigger by-elections and let residents have their say. I offered to resign my own Lancashire County Council seat too, despite my term not ending. None took up the offer.

## The 15 Wards

Every ward in Burnley will be contested. The seats up for election are those won in May 2022 (or subsequently filled by by-election). Burnley operates a thirds system where one of each ward's three councillors faces election in three out of every four years.

Cllr Hall won the Trinity by-election in October 2023. Since taking office his attendance is 73.8%, but it has deteriorated sharply this year to just 53%. He sits on Audit and Standards, Scrutiny and Licensing, but has not attended a single Audit and Standards meeting in the current municipal year. He had four absences without apology in the current year period alone.

## The Context

These councillors are paid a basic allowance of several thousand pounds per year from public funds to represent their wards. Attendance at meetings is the most visible and measurable part of the job. It is not the whole job, and there are legitimate reasons for occasional absence. But a pattern of non-attendance over a four-year term raises questions about whether residents are getting the representation they are paying for.

Voters in Gannow, Brunshaw and Trinity should ask their candidates on the doorstep: will you turn up?
