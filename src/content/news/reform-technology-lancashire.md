When Reform took control of Lancashire County Council in May 2025, we inherited more than a £28 million overspend and a £650 million roads backlog. We also inherited an organisation that had underinvested in technology for years. Paper-based processes. Legacy IT systems. A digital offering that was functional but outdated. That is changing.

Dave Dwyer, Reform's Cabinet Member for Data, Technology, Customer, and Efficiency, has led what he describes as "perhaps the largest financial exercise Lancashire County Council has undertaken in decades." The results so far tell their own story.

## The Digital Front Door: £4.3 Million Platform

In late 2025, LCC awarded a £4.3 million contract to Netcall for a new AI-powered customer interaction platform. The goal is straightforward: make it easier for Lancashire residents to contact the council and get answers quickly.

Dwyer put it simply: "This platform will help us to create smoother, more intuitive interactions with the council." The system uses AI to route enquiries, handle routine requests automatically, and free up human staff for cases that actually need a person. It is the kind of investment that reduces costs in the medium term while improving the service from day one.

## 1,400 Officers Using AI

This is not about buying one platform and putting out a press release. LCC now has 1,400 officers using AI tools at an advanced level, with Microsoft Copilot deployed across the organisation. That means staff can draft correspondence faster, analyse data more efficiently, and spend less time on administrative processes that add no value for residents.

The previous administration did not invest in this. The Conservatives ran LCC for twelve years and left behind manual processes that wasted staff time and taxpayer money. Reform's approach is different: train staff properly, give them the right tools, and hold them accountable for results.

## AI on the Roads

The old way of finding potholes was simple: wait for someone to phone in and complain. Lancashire has over 7,400 kilometres of road. The previous administration's manual inspection system captured less than 5% of actual defects. They did not even know what they were not fixing.

Reform deployed AI-powered sensors on bin lorries. These vehicles already travel every residential road in Lancashire on a regular cycle. Now they scan the road surface as they go, automatically detecting and categorising defects: potholes, cracking, edge deterioration, drainage failures. The data feeds directly into a prioritisation system that schedules repairs by severity, traffic volume, and risk.

The result: data-driven prioritisation replaces guesswork. Prevention instead of reaction. Defects down 42% in twelve months. Repair costs halved. Average repair size tripled from 2m&sup2; to 6m&sup2;, because when you know where every defect is, you fix the area, not just the hole someone complained about.

## SEND Transport: Technology Cutting Costs

One of the biggest wins has been in Special Educational Needs and Disabilities (SEND) transport. Using route optimisation technology and better data analysis, LCC has reduced the cost per passenger from approximately &pound;11,000 to &pound;8,000. That is a 30% reduction.

SEND transport is one of the fastest-growing cost pressures on every council in England. Reducing the per-passenger cost by 30% without reducing service quality is exactly the kind of efficiency that technology makes possible when it is applied with purpose.

## Digital Strategy 2025-2029

Reform is not doing this ad hoc. LCC's Digital Strategy 2025-2029, developed under Dwyer's leadership, sets out six core ambitions for transforming how the council uses technology:

1. **Digital-first services** for residents and businesses
2. **Data-driven decision making** across all departments
3. **AI-assisted operations** to reduce manual workload
4. **Cybersecurity resilience** to protect resident data
5. **Digital skills development** for the entire workforce
6. **Open and transparent** digital infrastructure

Every one of these ambitions has concrete delivery plans behind it. This is not a wish list. It is a programme of work with timelines, budgets, and accountability.

## Samlesbury Innovation Hub: &pound;13.2 Million

LCC's &pound;13.2 million investment in the Samlesbury Innovation Hub positions Lancashire at the centre of advanced manufacturing and digital technology in the North West. The hub provides workspace, R&D facilities, and collaboration opportunities for technology businesses, directly supporting high-value job creation in the county.

This is the kind of infrastructure investment that generates economic returns for decades. It creates skilled employment, attracts private sector investment, and keeps Lancashire competitive in an economy that increasingly rewards counties that invest in technology.

## The Efficiency Review

Dwyer's efficiency review sits alongside the technology programme. He described it as "perhaps the largest financial exercise Lancashire County Council has undertaken in decades." The review examined every department, every contract, every process for waste and inefficiency.

The result: **&pound;62 million in identified savings**, approximately &pound;5 for every &pound;100 the council spends. These savings are being delivered through contract renegotiation, vacancy management, procurement reform, and the elimination of duplicate processes. Technology is the enabler: you cannot find waste you cannot see, and you cannot see it without the right systems.

## What the Conservatives Left Behind

The contrast is stark. The Conservative administration ran LCC for twelve years and:

- Left a **&pound;28 million overspend** that Reform has since reduced to &pound;6.1 million
- Concealed an estimated **&pound;350 million** in bond portfolio losses
- Did not invest in digital transformation
- Let savings delivery collapse from 91% to 48%
- Left manual paper-based processes across critical services

Reform inherited all of this. In ten months, the direction has reversed.
