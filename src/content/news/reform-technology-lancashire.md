When Reform took control of Lancashire County Council in May 2025, we inherited more than a £28 million overspend and a £650 million roads backlog. We inherited a culture of opacity. Decisions made behind closed doors. Data buried in spreadsheets nobody could access. Financial losses concealed from elected members for years.

Ten months later, the culture is changing. And technology is at the heart of it.

## AI on Every Road

The old way of finding potholes was simple: wait for someone to phone in and complain. Lancashire has over 7,400 kilometres of road. The previous administration's manual inspection system captured less than 5% of actual defects. They did not even know what they were not fixing.

Reform deployed AI-powered sensors on bin lorries. These vehicles already travel every residential road in Lancashire on a regular cycle. Now they scan the road surface as they go, automatically detecting and categorising defects &mdash; potholes, cracking, edge deterioration, drainage failures. The data feeds directly into a prioritisation system that schedules repairs by severity, traffic volume, and risk.

The result: data-driven prioritisation replaces guesswork. Prevention instead of reaction. Defects down 42% in twelve months. Repair costs halved. Average repair size tripled from 2m&sup2; to 6m&sup2; &mdash; because when you know where every defect is, you fix the area, not just the hole someone complained about.

This is what technology looks like when it is applied with purpose, not purchased for a press release.

The AI DOGE platform &mdash; built with open data and available free at [aidoge.co.uk](https://aidoge.co.uk) &mdash; takes every council's raw spending data and makes it searchable, sortable, and analysable by anyone. You can look up any supplier, any department, any category. You can see trends over time. You can compare councils side by side.

But it goes further than simple transparency. The platform runs automated forensic analysis across every transaction: duplicate payment detection, split payment evasion screening, Benford's Law statistical testing, supplier concentration analysis, and Companies House cross-referencing. It flags anomalies that would take auditors weeks to find manually.

This is not a gimmick. This is accountability infrastructure. Every pound of public money should be traceable, and now it is.

## Councillor Integrity Screening

Conflicts of interest in local government are an open secret. Councillors with undeclared business interests. Directors of companies that receive council contracts. Family connections that never appear in the register of interests.

Reform built an automated integrity screening system that cross-references every councillor across all 15 Lancashire councils against Companies House directorship records, Electoral Commission donation data, FCA registers, and insolvency records. It checks for co-directorships between councillors and council suppliers. It looks for familial connections, temporal overlaps between business activity and council decisions, and patterns that no manual review would ever catch.

No other party in Lancashire is doing this. No other party in Lancashire has built the tools to do this.

## Data-Driven Budget Decisions

When Reform inherited LCC's budget, the previous administration had concealed an estimated **£350 million** in bond portfolio losses. The £28 million overspend was only the headline figure. Beneath it lay years of financial decisions that had never been properly scrutinised.

Reform's approach was different. Every budget line was challenged. Every contract was reviewed. The result: a **£1.33 billion** budget delivered at a council tax rise of **3.80%** &mdash; the lowest in a decade &mdash; while increasing highways investment by 45% and maintaining frontline services.

Technology made this possible. When you can see every transaction, every supplier relationship, every cost trend &mdash; across every department, across multiple years &mdash; you find the savings that manual processes miss. You find the contracts that were renewed without competitive tender. You find the spending patterns that no one questioned because no one could see them.

## The Bigger Picture

This is not about technology for its own sake. It is about a fundamental shift in how local government operates.

The old model &mdash; the one the Conservatives ran for twelve years and Labour runs at national level &mdash; relies on opacity. Decisions are made in closed meetings. Data is published in formats designed to be technically compliant but practically unreadable. Financial problems are concealed until they become crises. Residents are told what the council has decided, not given the tools to scrutinise those decisions themselves.

Reform's model is the opposite. Every pound traceable. Every councillor screened. Every road monitored. Every decision backed by data that residents can access for free.
